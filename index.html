1 · Global position

Where does Australia sit in the future cardiovascular crisis?

The map places Australia within the global pattern of projected CVD mortality, showing where the future burden is expected to concentrate.

  • Highest projected mortality is concentrated across Central Asia.
  • Australia remains comparatively low, but CVD is still nationally significant.

2 · Why CVD still matters

Has cardiovascular disease remained a dominant killer over time?

Historical mortality patterns show whether cardiovascular disease has remained a persistent burden compared with other major disease groups.

  • CVD remains one of the largest mortality categories across the full historical period.
  • This explains why future prevention is still important, even in lower-rate countries.

3 · Future pathways

Are countries improving at the same pace by 2050?

Selected countries show different projected mortality pathways from 2025 to 2050.

  • Australia remains low compared with selected major countries.
  • Higher-rate countries still carry much heavier projected mortality.

4 · Regional inequality

Do regional averages hide country-level differences?

Country-level dots and continental medians reveal how widely projected mortality varies within each region.

  • Asia shows wide internal variation, meaning regional labels hide inequality.
  • Australia is shown as a reference point rather than grouped into Oceania.

5 · What types of CVD drive deaths?

Which cardiovascular diseases make up the future burden?

Projected cardiovascular deaths are concentrated in a small number of disease categories.

  • Ischemic heart disease and stroke together account for about three quarters of projected CVD deaths.

6 · Preventable drivers

Which risk factors are the clearest prevention targets?

Modifiable risk factors shift the story from disease burden to prevention action.

  • Risk factors shift the story from disease burden to prevention action.
  • The largest attributable risks should be read as priority areas for intervention.

7 · Prevention and outcomes

Do stronger prevention systems align with lower mortality?

The bubble plot connects risk, prevention and mortality by comparing prevention scores with projected mortality rates.

  • Higher prevention scores generally align with lower projected mortality.
  • Bubble size shows that total deaths still matter, not just rates.

8 · System readiness

Which health systems look better prepared for future CVD burden?

Preparedness is compared across prevention, treatment, rehabilitation and health-pressure indicators.

  • Preparedness is uneven across prevention, treatment and rehabilitation.
  • Health pressure is reverse-scored, so darker means better preparedness.

9 · Patient-level risk

Do heavier smokers show elevated blood pressure earlier?

After the global and health-system view, the dashboard zooms into patient-level risk patterns to show how lifestyle factors appear in individual clinical data.

  • Heavy smokers show higher average systolic blood pressure in several older age groups.
  • This chart shows association, not causation.

10 · High-risk patient profile

Which lifestyle and clinical factors separate high-risk groups?

Relative differences between low- and high-risk patients act as the final patient-level profile.

  • Positive values are higher among high-risk patients.
  • Exercise and sleep sit lower, while cholesterol, triglycerides and stress sit higher.

Final takeaway

Cardiovascular risk is not only a future mortality problem.

It is a prevention, inequality and preparedness problem. Australia appears comparatively protected in global projections, but the dashboard shows why prevention still matters: disease burden is concentrated in ischemic heart disease and stroke, modifiable risks remain major drivers, and health systems vary in how prepared they are to respond.
